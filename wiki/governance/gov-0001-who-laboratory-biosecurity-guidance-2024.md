# WHO Laboratory Biosecurity Guidance 2024

## Status and scope

WHO published this global `Guidance (normative)` on 21 June 2024. It is a
complete revision of its 2006 guidance and applies a risk- and evidence-based
approach to high-consequence and other biosecurity-relevant material,
technology and information at laboratory, institutional and national levels.

The guidance targets institutions, national regulators, laboratory leadership,
IBCs, biosafety/biosecurity officers, personnel, funders and publishers. Its
horizontal scope includes collection, transport, processing, storage and
destruction in diagnostic, research and manufacturing-related laboratory
settings.

## Defined terms

WHO draws these source-specific boundaries:

- **biosafety:** prevention of unintentional exposure and inadvertent release;
- **laboratory biosecurity:** protection, control and accountability to prevent
  unauthorized access, loss, theft, misuse, diversion, release or weaponization
  of relevant material, technology and information;
- **biological risk management:** umbrella/continuum combining biosafety and
  biosecurity;
- **cybersecurity:** availability, integrity, authentication and confidentiality
  of digital systems/information, explicitly including laboratory equipment and
  building systems in laboratory context.

## Binding character

The instrument is global guidance and does not state its own legal binding
force. Wiki therefore does not infer universal binding effect. Duties may arise
where an applicable treaty, law, regulation, licence, contract or institutional
rule adopts relevant requirements. Source words such as `must` or `mandatory`
preserve WHO's prescriptive modality but are not silently relabelled as legal
obligations.

Annex 3 is a set of non-endorsed country examples. It cannot establish current
Australian, Canadian, Chinese, EU, Indian, Russian or US law without separate
primary-source and temporal verification.

## Institutional governance

The recommended institutional programme contains:

- a policy defining objectives, scope, roles, communication and code-of-conduct
  elements proportionate to activities and risk;
- an independent multidisciplinary IBC with documented review, conflict-of-
  interest safeguards and authority appropriate to local governance;
- a biosafety/biosecurity officer with defined competence, independence,
  reporting line and authority;
- documented assessment, approval, monitoring, incident follow-up, training,
  audit and periodic review;
- coordination between biological safety, security, information technology,
  bioinformatics, facilities/engineering, ethics and senior leadership.

## Two-tier national model

WHO proposes a hybrid risk/list-based model:

1. an institution/IBC identifies and reviews local high-consequence work,
   implements risk-informed controls and reports relevant findings;
2. a national authority defines risk-informed oversight, maintains a living
   restricted-material/equipment framework, supports institutions and receives
   feedback that can trigger review;
3. knowledge, incident and research feedback connect the two levels.

## Requirements or recommendations

At a high level WHO recommends:

- systematic, site-specific biosafety and biosecurity assessment before and
  during relevant work;
- risk/benefit consideration and proportionate locally sustainable controls;
- inventory, accountability and lifecycle records;
- information/cybersecurity protection for data, connected equipment and
  building/security systems;
- role-based competence, lawful/proportionate personnel safeguards and protected
  reporting;
- incident/near-miss reporting, investigation, corrective action and exercises;
- authorized transfer/transport and accountable receipt/closeout;
- review after change, new information, incidents or control evidence.

## Cyberbiosecurity relevance

The instrument directly connects information and cyber controls to biological
material/accountability, laboratory equipment, facility automation, continuity,
incident response and regulatory reporting. It therefore provides current
normative scope for cyber-bio dependencies, not proof of a specific attack path.

Its emerging-technology sections also place synthetic biology, gene editing, AI,
DIY biology and publication governance inside risk assessment. These are
potential-risk categories; they do not establish equivalent likelihood or
observed misuse.

## Exceptions and interpretation limits

- WHO laboratory biosecurity must not be silently extended to environmental or
  agricultural meanings of `biosecurity` (§1.1, printed p. 3).
- Consequence-focused assessment does not eliminate the need to document
  uncertainty or claim likelihood only with evidence.
- Personnel-screening recommendations require independent reconciliation with
  privacy, equality, labour, human-rights and due-process law; Table 6.1 detail
  is not operationalized here.
- Annex emergency and transport templates require local safety/engineering and
  legal validation. They are not universal instructions.
- The source's password box is not treated as current technical best practice.

## Evidence and uncertainty

WHO reports expert working groups, two review rounds and declarations-of-interest
screening. It does not report a systematic search, evidence grading,
implementation cohort or comparative effectiveness evaluation. Cyberattack
context cites `SRC-0002`, so that threat claim is not independent corroboration;
the normative control recommendations are a distinct WHO lineage.
