# US FDA medical-device cybersecurity governance 2026

## Current instrument and lineage

The February 2026 Level 2 revision is FDA's current final guidance checked on
2026-07-12 and supersedes the June 2025 edition. The reused PDF media identifier
makes the source hash part of the governance record. No later correction,
withdrawal or replacement was located in the bounded official check.

## Legal modality

The guidance's `should` statements are recommendations and permit an alternative
approach that satisfies applicable requirements. The text separately points to
QMSR and FD&C Act §524B duties. Those provisions retain their own actor,
device, submission and timing predicates; citing them does not turn every
guidance recommendation into an obligation or prove compliance.

> **Claim record — GOV-0007-C02 | source-report**
> **Claim:** The governance model has two preserved modalities—nonbinding FDA
> recommendations and separately scoped statutory/regulatory duties—so neither
> recommendation nor submitted documentation alone establishes compliance.
> **Claim status:** active
> **Scope:** Modality expressed by the guidance; not legal advice or a complete
> codification of QMSR/§524B.
> **As of:** 2026-07-12.
> **Review after:** 2026-10-10.
> **Evidence status:** single-source
> **Confidence:** high
> **Evidence:** [SRC-0017](../sources/src-0017-fda-medical-device-cybersecurity-2026.md),
> introductory notice and §I, printed pp. 1–2 (PDF pp. 5–6); §IV.A,
> printed pp. 5–7 (PDF pp. 9–11); §VII.A–C, printed pp. 31–34
> (PDF pp. 35–38).
> **Basis / limits:** FDA states the recommendation/duty distinction directly.
> Applicability depends on current law and product/submission facts.

## Actors, products and decisions

The architecture connects manufacturers or other submitters, FDA review
centres, healthcare facilities, providers, patients/caregivers, servicers,
software/component suppliers and security researchers. Premarket decisions,
labeling, postmarket monitoring, coordinated disclosure, updates, servicing and
end-of-support communication allocate different responsibilities. This is not
an organization-specific RACI or evidence that the dependencies operate.

> **Claim record — GOV-0007-C03 | source-report**
> **Claim:** FDA allocates device-cybersecurity lifecycle responsibilities
> across submitters/manufacturers, FDA, users/care settings, patients, servicers,
> suppliers and vulnerability reporters rather than assigning security to one
> product actor.
> **Claim status:** active
> **Scope:** Actor classes in the guidance, not an entity-specific duty or
> operational responsibility matrix.
> **As of:** 2026-02-03.
> **Review after:** 2027-01-08.
> **Evidence status:** single-source
> **Confidence:** high
> **Evidence:** [SRC-0017](../sources/src-0017-fda-medical-device-cybersecurity-2026.md),
> §III, printed pp. 3–5 (PDF pp. 7–9); §§V–VII, printed pp. 9–37
> (PDF pp. 13–41).
> **Basis / limits:** Actor and lifecycle responsibilities are direct. Actual
> contracts, handoffs, submissions and enforcement are absent.

## Total-product-lifecycle governance

FDA places cybersecurity within device safety/effectiveness and quality
management from design through manufacturing, submission, deployment,
monitoring, update, servicing and retirement. Security risk, safety risk,
architecture, controls, tests, labeling, SBOM and postmarket plans are linked
but remain distinct evidence objects.

> **Claim record — GOV-0007-C04 | analytic-judgment**
> **Claim:** The guidance defines a traceable total-product-lifecycle governance
> chain from risk/design through submission and postmarket correction, while
> preserving separate safety, security, product and user responsibilities.
> **Claim status:** active
> **Scope:** Structural interpretation of FDA's guidance, not a complete device
> quality system or an approved submission.
> **As of:** 2026-07-12.
> **Review after:** 2027-01-08.
> **Evidence status:** inferred
> **Confidence:** high
> **Evidence:** [SRC-0017](../sources/src-0017-fda-medical-device-cybersecurity-2026.md),
> §§IV–VII, printed pp. 5–37 (PDF pp. 9–41); Appendices 1 and 4,
> printed pp. 38–46, 51–53 (PDF pp. 42–50, 55–57).
> **Basis / limits:** Lifecycle elements and traceability expectations are
> direct; the compact governance chain is editorial and not adoption evidence.

## Assurance and implementation boundary

FDA recommends or requires, in scoped contexts, documentation, verification,
validation, security testing, independent-tester disclosure and postmarket
plans. This is assurance design. The guidance provides no manufacturer
submission, clearance decision, inspection, test result, audit, field response
or causal effectiveness evaluation.

> **Claim record — GOV-0007-C05 | analytic-judgment**
> **Claim:** FDA's documentation, testing and review architecture does not
> establish that any represented safeguard was implemented, tested successfully,
> effective or independently evaluated in deployment.
> **Claim status:** active
> **Scope:** Evidence maturity of this transaction; not a finding that FDA or
> manufacturers lack such records elsewhere.
> **As of:** 2026-07-12.
> **Review after:** 2027-01-08.
> **Evidence status:** inferred
> **Confidence:** high
> **Evidence:** [SRC-0017](../sources/src-0017-fda-medical-device-cybersecurity-2026.md),
> §§V.C and VII.E, printed pp. 25–27, 36–37 (PDF pp. 29–31, 40–41);
> Appendix 4, printed pp. 51–53 (PDF pp. 55–57); `SRC-0017-C08/C10`.
> **Basis / limits:** Requested assurance artifacts and absence of result data
> in the guidance are direct. Separate product/regulator evidence could change
> implementation or effectiveness status.
