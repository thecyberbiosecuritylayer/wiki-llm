# US–EU human-omics and electronic-health-data governance reconciliation

## Comparison boundary

This synthesis compares only the directly reviewed U.S. Data Security Program
and EU EHDS sources, with NIST supplying separate U.S. genomic cybersecurity/
privacy scope. It does not claim legal equivalence, extraterritorial coverage,
mutual recognition or compliance transfer.

| Dimension | U.S. DOJ DSP | EU EHDS | Reconciliation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Primary object | Defined access transactions involving government-related or bulk U.S. sensitive personal data | Electronic health data primary use, EHR systems and secondary use | Different regulatory objects |
| Human-omics limb | Thresholded human-omic data and derivable biospecimens; specified paths prohibited | Broad secondary-use categories including multiple omics/biobank data | Category overlap does not make duties equivalent |
| Direction/actors | U.S.-person transaction giving country/covered-person access | Natural persons, professionals/providers, holders/users, Member States/Commission and product actors | Different actor and rights structures |
| Controls | Prohibition or restricted-transaction authorization with CISA/Part 202 duties | Rights, EHR conformity, permit/prohibited-use, secure environment and infrastructure duties | Both binding, not cross-compliance baselines |
| Timing | Effective and affirmative duties already applicable | In force, but staged application through 2027–2035 | Entry/effect/application/adoption kept separate |
| Evidence maturity | Legal design; implementation/audit/enforcement outcomes absent | Legal design and one Board act; deployments/audit/effectiveness absent | Neither supplies SF3 outcome evidence |

> **Claim record — SYN-0004-C01 | analytic-judgment**
> **Claim:** DOJ DSP and EHDS regulate overlapping human-omics/health-data
> territory through materially different objects, actors and modalities—U.S.
> covered-access transactions versus EU primary use/EHR systems/secondary use—
> and cannot be treated as compliance equivalents.
> **Claim status:** active
> **Scope:** High-level comparison of reviewed current legal texts; not legal
> advice or conflict-of-laws analysis.

## Human-omics scope reconciliation

NIST distinguishes human and non-human genomic cybersecurity/privacy contexts.
DOJ defines four human-omic classes and exclusions for one U.S. access regime.
EHDS adds genetic/genomic, epigenomic, proteomic, transcriptomic, metabolomic,
lipidomic, pathogen, cohort and biobank categories for EU secondary use. The
lists overlap but have different purposes and boundaries.

> **Claim record — SYN-0004-C02 | analytic-judgment**
> **Claim:** Independent NIST, DOJ and EHDS lineages jointly support a broad
> human/non-human genomics and multi-omics scope while preserving that DOJ and
> EHDS category lists are regulation-specific rather than a universal ontology.
> **Claim status:** active
> **Scope:** Comparative domain scope, not dataset classification under either
> regulation.

## Prohibitions, permissions and exceptions

DOJ prohibits defined brokerage and human-omics/biospecimen access paths while
conditionally authorizing other restricted transactions. EHDS permits only
specified secondary purposes through a request/permit path, separately
prohibits named uses and permits bounded opt-out/public-interest exceptions.
Clinical/research references in both instruments are condition-specific.

> **Claim record — SYN-0004-C04 | analytic-judgment**
> **Claim:** DOJ transaction prohibitions/restrictions/exceptions and EHDS
> purpose/permit/prohibited-use/opt-out mechanisms interrupt different edges;
> neither a DOJ safeguard nor an EHDS permitted purpose is an unconditional
> authorization or evidence of acceptable privacy risk.
> **Claim status:** active
> **Scope:** Cross-regime modality reconciliation, not application to a person,
> dataset, study or transaction.
> **As of:** 2026-07-12.
> **Review after:** 2026-10-10.
> **Evidence status:** inferred

## Accountability architecture

The U.S. regime connects U.S. persons, counterparties, DOJ/NSD, CISA,
transaction services, auditors and reporting/enforcement paths. EHDS connects
natural persons, providers/professionals, product suppliers, national
authorities/contact points/access bodies, Member States, Commission, Board and
two cross-border infrastructures. Both have supplier/service and national
accountability dependencies, but only EHDS creates EU primary/secondary health-
data infrastructures.

| Accountability class | U.S. DOJ DSP dependency | EU EHDS dependency | Preserved boundary |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Institutional | U.S. persons/counterparties ↔ DOJ/NSD, CISA, auditors and reporting/enforcement functions | persons/providers/holders/users ↔ digital-health, access and market-surveillance bodies | Different rights, duties and enforcement objects |
| National | federal rulemaking, designation, licensing and enforcement responsibilities | Member State authorities/contact points ↔ Commission and EHDS Board coordination | EU direct applicability/staging is not U.S. implementation evidence |
| Supplier/service | vendors, transaction services and covered-person access dependencies | EHR suppliers, health-data holders/users and secure-processing providers | Supplier role never establishes conformity or effective control |
| Cross-border | regulated country/covered-person access, which need not involve physical data export | `MyHealth@EU` and `HealthData@EU` contact-point/infrastructure responsibilities | Access regulation and cross-border infrastructure are not equivalent mechanisms |

> **Claim record — SYN-0004-C05 | analytic-judgment**

> governance architecture spanning institutional, national, supplier/service
> and cross-border accountability dependencies while preserving their
> different access, infrastructure and actor models.

## Unresolved gaps

- No national adoption/readiness or operational connection inventory is
  included.
- No product conformity, audit, enforcement or effectiveness outcome is
  compared.
- U.S. general health-data/clinical-device governance remains incomplete;
  DOJ DSP is not an EHDS analogue.
- Conflict-of-laws, adequacy, national exceptions and sector-specific overlay
  analysis require additional primary instruments and legal expertise.
