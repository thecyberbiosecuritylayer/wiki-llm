# Normative boundaries among biosafety, biosecurity, and cybersecurity

> Which definitions and rules for distinguishing biosafety, biosecurity, and
> cybersecurity should a global comparative wiki use, and where must it
> preserve jurisdictional or institutional differences?

## Scope

The question concerns the terminological boundaries needed to model
cyberbiosecurity. It does not assume that a single formulation will be universal
across organizations, sectors, and jurisdictions.

## Current evidence

Murch et al. briefly describe biosecurity as reducing risks associated with
misuse of science that could cause harm through the creation, production, and
intentional **or accidental** release of infectious-disease pathogens or their
by-products, such as toxins. This source-specific formulation includes
accidental release, whereas the wiki's editorial convention classifies
accidental exposure, release, or harm under biosafety
([SRC-0001](../sources/src-0001-murch-cyberbiosecurity-2018.md), § Background,
first paragraph).

Crawford et al. restate WHO 2020 and distinguish biosecurity as measures against
unauthorized access to, loss, theft, misuse, diversion, or release of biological
materials, and biosafety as measures against unintentional exposure or
inadvertent release. This is a useful secondary lineage
([SRC-0002](../sources/src-0002-crawford-high-containment-laboratories-2023.md),
§ Introduction, second paragraph).

Direct WHO 2024 guidance has now been integrated. It defines biosafety in terms
of unintentional exposure and inadvertent release; laboratory biosecurity in
terms of the protection, control, and accountability of material, technology,
and information; and explicitly extends cybersecurity to laboratory equipment
and building systems. At the same time, the WHO Glossary includes intentional
**or accidental unauthorized access**, while §§5.1 and 5.3 separately treat
accidental release or loss of biosecurity-relevant material as biosecurity
risks or incidents; biological risk management is presented as a continuum.
Thus, one international normative gap has been closed, but the direct source
shows overlap rather than an absolute boundary
([SRC-0004](../sources/src-0004-who-laboratory-biosecurity-guidance-2024.md),
Glossary, printed pp. xiii–xiv; §1, printed pp. 1–3; §§5.1, 5.3,
printed pp. 19–22).

## Resolution criteria

The question can be closed after the wiki:

1. supplements the integrated WHO laboratory guidance with current,
   authoritative definitions from representative jurisdictions and adjacent
   sectors;
2. compares whether they cover accidental harm, intentional misuse, digital
   dependencies, and cyber-physical consequences;
3. separates a shared core from jurisdictional or institutional differences;
   and
4. updates the glossary and
   [CON-0001](../concepts/con-0001-cyberbiosecurity.md) without presenting the
   editorial boundary as universal consensus.

## Evidence needed

- current definitions from representative jurisdictions;
- international and sectoral sources for agriculture, the environment,
  clinical data, and industrial biotechnology, where `biosecurity` may carry a
  different meaning; and
- documents that directly describe the intersection or allocation of
  responsibilities among biosafety, biosecurity, and cybersecurity.

## Evidence and uncertainty

The identified difference may result from distinct objectives and traditions
of terminology rather than a direct conflict over practical measures. WHO 2024
provides a direct international baseline, but the jurisdictional and sectoral
comparison is incomplete; question status: **open, partially resolved**.
