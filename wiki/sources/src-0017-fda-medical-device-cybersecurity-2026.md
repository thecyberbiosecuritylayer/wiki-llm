## Force and incorporated-duty boundary

The document repeatedly labels itself `Contains Nonbinding Recommendations`.
Its introduction says FDA guidance does not generally create enforceable
responsibilities and that `should` means suggested or recommended. It also
quotes or cites separate statutory and regulatory duties, especially the QMSR
and FD&C Act §524B for qualifying cyber-device submissions. Those duties apply
only through their own predicates; the guidance is not itself a statute or
evidence that a manufacturer complied.

## Device and clinical-system scope

The guidance covers devices with cybersecurity considerations, including
software, firmware or programmable logic; it is not limited to network-enabled
devices. It includes CDRH/CBER submission pathways, biological-product devices
and the device constituent of applicable combination products. `Cyber device`
under §524B is a narrower subset with separate predicates. FDA's medical-device
system includes the device and connected facility networks, other devices,
software-update servers and other dependencies.

> **Claim record — SRC-0017-C04 | source-report**
> **Claim:** FDA's guidance scope spans qualifying medical devices, including
> diagnostic and biological/combination-product device contexts and a narrower
> statutory cyber-device subset, within larger clinical/network/service
> systems.
> **Claim status:** active
> **Scope:** FDA device cybersecurity scope; not all laboratory software, EHRs,
> health data, biological products or healthcare providers.
> **As of:** 2026-02-03.
> **Review after:** 2027-01-08.

## Safety, lifecycle and system dependencies

FDA connects device cybersecurity to safety/effectiveness and possible delay or
denial of diagnosis/treatment. It recommends a Secure Product Development
Framework and security risk management across design, manufacturing,
distribution, postmarket monitoring, updates, servicing, end of support and
decommissioning. It treats security and safety risk assessments as linked but
distinct and explicitly recognizes that no device can be completely secure.

> **Claim record — SRC-0017-C05 | source-report**
> **Claim:** The guidance frames cybersecurity as a total-product-lifecycle
> device-safety dependency whose compromise can affect device function,
> diagnostic reporting or care availability, while rejecting zero-risk design.
> **Claim status:** active
> **Scope:** Potential device/system pathways and recommended lifecycle method;
> not a documented incident, likelihood estimate or universal patient outcome.
> **As of:** 2026-02-03.
> **Review after:** 2027-01-08.
> **Evidence status:** single-source
> **Confidence:** high
> **Evidence:** §§I–III, printed pp. 1–5 (PDF pp. 5–9); §§IV–V.A, printed
> pp. 5–19 (PDF pp. 9–23); Appendix 5, `Lifecycle`, printed p. 56
> (PDF p. 60).
> **Basis / limits:** The lifecycle and possible safety/care connection are
> direct. Cited historical events are secondary examples and do not establish
> the complete path for a named device or patient.

## Risk, architecture and traceability design

The recommended package includes separate safety/security risk assessment,
threat modeling, third-party component/SBOM review, unresolved-anomaly review,
architecture views, trust-boundary and use-case analysis, control-to-risk
traceability, labeling and postmarket management. Architecture documentation
is submission evidence of a manufacturer's design argument, not a deployed
topology verified by this source.

## Control categories

Appendix 1 supplies eight categories: authentication; authorization;
cryptography; code, data and execution integrity; confidentiality; event
detection and logging; resiliency and recovery; and firmware/software updates.
Elsewhere the guidance adds threat modeling, SBOM, transparency, vulnerability
management and assurance activities. The categories overlap but are not
identical to CSF/WHO function names.

## Testing and evidence-state boundary

FDA recommends security-requirement, threat-mitigation, vulnerability and
penetration testing, tester-independence disclosure and regular lifecycle
testing. It asks manufacturers to submit test evidence and discusses possible
patch metrics. The guidance itself contains no manufacturer test report,
metric value, comparator or independently evaluated effectiveness result.
