## Scope, method and manufacturing model

Q13 is a consensus normative guideline developed by an ICH Expert Working
Group and subjected to regulatory-party consultation. It is not a primary
facility study, systematic review, incident investigation or performance
evaluation. The main text covers development, implementation, operation and
lifecycle management of continuous manufacture for chemical drug substances/
products and therapeutic proteins, with possible application of its principles
to other biological or biotechnological entities.

It defines continuous manufacture functionally as continuous input, in-process
transformation and concomitant output. The guideline focuses on two or more
directly connected unit operations and distinguishes hybrid batch/continuous,
fully continuous drug-substance or drug-product, and integrated drug-substance/
drug-product modes. Its annexes provide illustrative chemical, tablet,
therapeutic-protein, integrated-process and disturbance examples.

> **Claim record — SRC-0031-C04 | source-report**
> **Claim:** Q13 directly covers new and converted existing processes for
> chemical entities and therapeutic proteins, distinguishes hybrid, fully
> continuous and integrated modes, and recognizes that a change in one
> connected unit operation may affect upstream/downstream operations and output
> material quality.
> **Claim status:** active
> **Scope:** Q13 continuous-manufacturing scope; other biological/
> biotechnological application is possible rather than automatically covered,

## Assets, data and system dependencies

Across the main text and Annex III, Q13 identifies input/raw materials, cell
bank vials, in-process material, output drug substance/product, equipment,
physical connections, digital control interfaces, sensors/PAT, automation,
process models, process/quality data, specifications, batch information, CTD
records and PQS-managed procedures. It also allocates roles to manufacturers/
applicants and regulatory authorities. Broader workforce, patient and supply-

## Bidirectional cyber-biological transfer structure

Q13 supplies both functional directions needed to understand the coupling,
without claiming cyberattack:

1. digital monitoring/model/control or automation state can adjust process
   parameters, pause operation, divert material or support release decisions,
   affecting physical process and product-quality state;
2. material attributes, equipment condition and process/PAT signals become
   digital data used to detect disturbances/drift and drive control, diversion,
   investigation and release decisions.

> **Claim record — SRC-0031-C06 | source-report**
> **Claim:** Q13 documents a two-way functional path between digital control/
> decision state and physical material/product state: automated controls act on
> the process, while material/process measurements feed digital control,
> diversion and quality-release decisions.
> **Claim status:** active
> **Scope:** Intended continuous-manufacturing operation and control design;
> not malicious access, successful manipulation, an observed event or proof
> that a safeguard interrupted an attack.
> **As of:** 2022-11-16.
> **Review after:** 2027-01-08.
> **Evidence status:** single-source
> **Confidence:** high
> **Evidence:** Part I §§3.1.4–3.1.7, printed pp. 4–6 (physical pp. 9–11);

## Hazard and control-state evidence

Q13 treats planned start-up/shutdown/pause and unplanned disturbances,
input-material variability, drift/trend, equipment fatigue, material aging,
component failure, sensor fouling, data gaps and contamination as conditions
that can affect state of control or output quality. It recommends a holistic
control strategy linking material characterization, equipment/system design,
monitoring/PAT, model validation, traceability, diversion, acceptance criteria,
investigation, CAPA and lifecycle management.

## Evidence conflict — FDA regional text

FDA's regional document preserves the full Q13 structure but is not a verbatim
facsimile. It changes layout/numbering, `guideline` to `guidance`, spelling and
some modal/editorial phrases. These FDA wordings must be cited to the FDA
version when their exact force matters rather than attributed verbatim to ICH
or EMA.

One difference is an apparent internal cross-reference defect. At FDA physical
p. 48/printed p. 42, Annex V §3.3 says that diverted amount is the same as
described in `section III.B (3.2) Annex IV`. The main ICH physical p. 46/
printed p. 41 and EMA physical p. 43 instead say `Section 3.2 of this annex`—
the immediately preceding Annex V §3.2. The document structure and matching
ICH/EMA wording identify Annex V §3.2 as the intended target. The FDA current
HTML has no corrigendum/erratum notice, so the defect is not silently edited.

## Limitations, conflicts and safety boundary

- Annex I–V examples are expressly illustrative/non-exhaustive. Phrases about
  commercial equipment, development studies or validation inside those
  examples do not identify a deployment, dataset or independent evaluator.
- The guideline does not document a cyber incident, exploited vulnerability,
  product-quality event, patient outcome, supply consequence or safeguard
  effectiveness result. It cannot create an `INC` or pass `BMO-CI/BMO-AE`.
- FDA and EMA regional texts are coordinated implementations of the same ICH
  technical lineage, not two independent scientific confirmations.
- Q13 incorporates or cites other ICH guidelines by reference. Their mention
  is not an ingest of their complete text or independent evidence.
- No author-level funding or conflict-of-interest statement appears in the
  harmonized guideline. The consensus regulator/industry ICH process is part
  of its provenance and should not be confused with independent evaluation.
- Wiki pages retain functional equipment/control classes only. They omit
  operational setpoints, process recipes and facility-specific topology; the
  source contains no exploit instructions or credentials.
