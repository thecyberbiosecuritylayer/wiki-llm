## Executive summary

The authors propose cyberbiosecurity as a distinct hybrid discipline at the
intersection of cybersecurity, cyber-physical security, and biosecurity. They
explicitly describe their definition as initial and expect it to evolve with
the field.

The article extends the discussion from the protection of data and
biotechnology to biomanufacturing. Using one test-bed facility as an example,
the authors treat biomanufacturing as an interdependent system comprising the
bioprocess, supply chain, information and cyber-physical infrastructure,
physical facility, and human factors. The publication, however, presents only
the top level of the analysis: it does not disclose a detailed method, the full
set of observations, or validation of the proposed measures' effectiveness.

## Scope and methods

The article covers the intellectual history of the term, reviews several US
workshops and publications, and gives a top-level description of a systems
analysis of a biomanufacturing facility. The authors report that the analysis
was conducted in stages, considered human factors and downstream
considerations, and was performed without compromising, damaging, or altering
the facility.

The publication is not a systematic review. For the facility analysis, it does
not provide a data-collection protocol, checklist of tests, sufficiency
criteria, quantitative results, or materials for independent reproduction.

## Limitations and conflicts

- The cyberbiosecurity definition is the authors' 2018 proposal and is
  explicitly labeled as initial.
- The brief, source-specific definition of biosecurity in § Background includes
  both intentional and accidental release. It overlaps with this wiki's
  editorial boundary for biosafety and does not replace a future normative
  comparison.
- Much of the context is US-centric; statements about the US bioeconomy,
  programs, and policy should not be transferred to other jurisdictions.
- The facility analysis is limited to a high-level description. It supports a
  systems framing but does not establish attack frequency, risk magnitude, or
  the effectiveness of a specific control.
- The authors declared no commercial or financial conflicts of interest (local
  artifact, § Conflict of interest statement). At the same time, the test-bed
  facility analysis was funded under a US Department of Defense / US Strategic
  Command contract with the National Strategic Research Institute; this is
  context for the commission and the analysis's stated objectives. The
  publication does not make it possible to determine the sponsor's influence on
  the findings (local artifact, § Footnotes, Funding).
- Figure 1 was inspected, but its pixel data are not part of the local raw
  snapshot.
