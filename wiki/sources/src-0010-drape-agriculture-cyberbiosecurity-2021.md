## Executive summary

Virginia Tech organized a two-day virtual workshop in October 2020 for people
from food/agriculture, cybersecurity, academia, government and law enforcement.
The authors qualitatively coded audio, chat and collaborative-document artifacts
and report four themes: challenges, solutions, viewpoints and vocabulary.
Participants discussed limited expertise and resources, unclear responsibility,
supply-chain dependencies, training needs and possible government roles.

For the wiki, the source supplies a first substantive but incomplete
agricultural cyberbiosecurity map: connected crop/livestock monitoring,
equipment and data exchange can be coupled to farm production and downstream
food supply. It also supplies a safely abstracted hypothetical path from
unreliable data or unavailable access to production/supply disruption and a set
of recommended capability/response measures. It supplies no observed attack,
measured biological or economic consequence, deployed safeguard, test result,
effectiveness evidence or current legal obligation.

## Scope and methods

The bounded system is US food and agriculture cyberbiosecurity as discussed at
one virtual workshop. Table 1 lists eight session topics and the source types
used in analysis. Invited audiences included producers, food manufacturers,
commodity boards, supporting businesses, cybersecurity/technology firms,
universities, government and law enforcement.

The three research questions ask about professional experience, responses to
new situations and recommendations for future work. Data came from concurrent
workshop artifacts rather than a representative farm survey or technical
assessment. Multiple modalities from the same workshop may enrich the corpus,
but do not create independent evidence lineages.

The work does not cover a full agricultural lifecycle, aquaculture/wildlife,
environmental monitoring, veterinary/plant diagnostic architecture, named
farms, system configurations, incident records, biological outcomes, control
deployment or assurance metrics.

## Limitations and conflicts

- Approximately 170 registrations must not be treated as approximately 170
  analytic participants; the paper separately says data came from
  approximately 80 participants.
- Attendance varied by session, and the authors say they could not remain
  informed about which organizations were represented or whose voices entered
  each session.
- `213 challenges` are code instances, not incident, participant or unique-
  threat counts.
- Only selected quotations are published. The paper does not report inter-coder
  agreement, saturation, participant-level denominators, pre/post effects,
  technical validation or control outcomes.
- Anonymous participant statements cannot be promoted to independent official,
  industry or law-enforcement disclosures.
- Threat/consequence statements in the Introduction are mainly literature
  synthesis. Direct cited studies must be ingested before treating them as
  independent empirical support.
- Randall Murch also co-authored the already ingested 2018 framing source, and
  this paper explicitly relies on Murch/Duncan-related literature. The 2020
  workshop is a distinct dataset but remains within the Virginia Tech
  programme lineage; it is not independent corroboration of `SRC-0001` for
  definitions or general claims.
- The project was supported by USDA-NIFA grant `2019-67021-29,956`, accession
  `1019771`; the workshop was also co-sponsored by Virginia's Commonwealth
  CyberInitiative, Tyson Foods, Virginia Tech College of Agriculture and Life
  Sciences and Virginia Agricultural Experiment Station (`#s10`, line 1709).
  The authors declare no commercial or financial conflict (`#s11`, line 1710).
  Sponsorship is provenance context, not evidence of bias or independence.
