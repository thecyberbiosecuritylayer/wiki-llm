## Scope and model taxonomy

The committee scopes AI-enabled biological tools to biology-specific or
scientific AI, including genomics/other omics, biological prediction and
design, synthetic biology, drug discovery, biosurveillance and related
research/clinical decision uses. Generic chatbots are outside the central
technical scope unless connected to biological capabilities. `Biological
data` mainly means public DNA, RNA, protein and omics data; health/medical data
are excluded unless a section explicitly brings them in. The report is
unclassified and expressly is not a full threat assessment.

Chapter 2 distinguishes foundation, predictive, generative/design and
scientific-assistant functions, while Appendix A supplies a more detailed
author-attributed taxonomy. The examples and model inventory are a technical
snapshot based predominantly on 2024 and early-2025 evidence, not a current
catalogue of July 2026 capabilities, releases or safeguards.

## Data, model and experimental lifecycle

The committee uses the Design–Build–Test–Learn (DBTL) frame. Biological data,
scientific knowledge or an idea enter digital analysis/design; candidate
outputs are selected; physical build and experimental test remain a gate; the
resulting data can feed later learning. AI presently contributes most strongly
to digital ideation, analysis and design. It does not generally remove the
physical production and validation bottleneck, and current automated
laboratories remain application-specific, resource-intensive and human-
dependent.

Chapter 5 separately maps a research-data lifecycle around plan/design,
collect/create, analyze/collaborate, evaluate/archive, share/disseminate and
publish/reuse, with storage/management at the centre. Figure 5-1 visually names
metadata, electronic laboratory notebooks, version control, reproducibility,
retention/destruction, IP and roles, but it is a reproduced third-party data
framework—not a complete model lifecycle. Model deployment, monitoring/update
and retirement are not reconciled end to end.

## Vulnerability and consequence taxonomy

The report separates malicious misuse of model capability from corruption or
failure within the AI/data/software chain. Defensive high-level cases include
noisy, biased, incomplete or intentionally manipulated data; training-
data poisoning; hidden model behavior; safeguard bypass; malicious fine-
tuning/instruction manipulation; open-source software supply-chain exposure;
recursively generated-data/model-collapse effects; limited generalization;
and unreliable automation. Potential outcomes include erroneous model outputs,
misdirected research, delayed countermeasure work, unsafe physical or clinical
decisions and deliberate misuse. These are threat/hazard hypotheses and cited
examples, not frequencies or observed incidents.

Appendix A's train/test `data leakage` means evaluation contamination between
training and held-out sets. It is not confidentiality exfiltration. The report
does not use a literal model/data `drift` taxonomy; time-changing biological
phenotypes and model-update concerns must not be silently relabeled as a
complete drift treatment.

## Control and assurance functions

Recommended functions include provenance linking creators, data, tools,
software dependencies and training-data lineage; curation and quality checks;
fine-grained access and sandboxes; privacy-preserving analysis; program
analysis, automated package testing and malicious-package detection; screening
and logging around biological-design/procurement interfaces; red teaming,
digital twins and anomaly detection; continuous penetration testing; robust
training; standardized benchmarks; regular automated and expert audits; human
scientific review; experimental validation; and capability-triggered
reassessment in a sandbox.

The report also records limits: controls can block beneficial work, screening
can produce false positives or fail against sophisticated actors, proprietary
APIs can obscure safeguards, benchmarks can be simplistic or lack interpretable
meaning, and model evaluation science is nascent. Recovery after compromised
state is not a complete committee control family. Candidate methods are not
implementation, successful test or effectiveness evidence.

## Advisory recommendations and governance boundary

The committee recommends continued infectious-agent research and
biosurveillance; investment in AI-enabled countermeasure development; an
emergency public–private compute/tool partnership; continual `if–then`
capability assessment; AI-compatible datasets; robust data provenance and
curation; and sustainable national stewardship infrastructure. Named actors
include DoD, HHS/NIH/CDC/BARDA/ARPA-H, USDA, DOE, DHS, NSF/NAIRR and public–
private partners.

These are expert recommendations, not enacted duties, appropriations, agency
adoption, international harmonization or evidence that cited 2024 U.S. policy
remains the current 2026 instrument set. The report supplies one U.S. advisory
line and does not reconcile current AI/data/genomics/research/device rules in
two jurisdictions.
