# Laboratory biosecurity guidance

## Bibliographic identity

World Health Organization. *Laboratory biosecurity guidance*. Geneva: WHO,
2024. Electronic ISBN `978-92-4-009511-3`; print ISBN
`978-92-4-009512-0`. The official publication page is dated 21 June 2024 and
classifies the document as `Guidance (normative)`. The artifact calls itself an
update to the 2006 guidance and a `complete revision of the 1st edition`; it
does not use the label “second edition”.

In the wiki, this is `normative-guidance`: authoritative global WHO guidance,
but not a self-executing law, an implementation survey, or an effectiveness
evaluation.

## Executive summary

WHO describes laboratory biosecurity as a risk- and evidence-based program for
the protection, control, and accountability of biological materials,
technologies, information, and related resources. The guidance combines three
vertical levels—laboratory, institution, and national regulatory body—with a
horizontal lifecycle from collection and processing through transport and
storage to destruction.

The document proposes a five-step assessment cycle, institutional governance
through policy, an IBC, and a biosafety/biosecurity officer, two-tier national
oversight, and families of physical, personnel, inventory, information and
cybersecurity, transport, and incident-response controls. These are
recommendations and templates, not evidence that controls were implemented,
tested, or effective.

## Scope and methods

WHO Laboratory Biosafety and Biosecurity Programme developed the revision with
TAG-B working groups and additional subject-matter experts; the draft underwent
two reviews. The document combines WHO policy positions, expert-developed input
and review, selected literature, templates and examples. It does not describe a systematic
review, GRADE-like evidence appraisal, facility sample or implementation study.

Its intended audiences include institutions, IBCs, biosafety/biosecurity
officers, researchers, national regulators, funders and publishers. Scope spans
diagnostic, research, mobile, manufacturing/pharmaceutical and related
laboratory settings, but remains laboratory-biosecurity guidance rather than a
complete standard for every clinical, agricultural, environmental or industrial
workflow.

## Limitations and conflicts

- Guidance is advisory. Words such as `must`, `mandatory` and `need to` express
  WHO's recommended modality; they are not universal legal duties without
  jurisdictional adoption.
- No adoption, implementation, testing or effectiveness sample is reported.
  Templates ask users to evaluate controls; this is not evidence that evaluation
  occurred.
- §6.5 cites Crawford et al. 2023 (`SRC-0002`) for cyberattack context, so WHO
  does not provide an independent empirical lineage for that claim.
- Annex 3 explicitly gives non-endorsed national examples and relies on dated
  references. It is not authoritative current law; every jurisdiction requires
  separate primary legal ingest.
- WHO definitions show overlap: the Glossary includes intentional or accidental
  unauthorized access under biosecurity, while §§5.1 and 5.3 also treat
  accidental release/loss of biosecurity-relevant material as biosecurity
  risks/incidents (printed pp. 19–22; PDF pp. 39–42). Biosafety covers
  unintentional exposure/inadvertent release. Direct evidence narrows, but does
  not close, `QUE-0001`.
- The source's consequence-focused option for biosecurity risk assessment is a
  methodological choice, not a universal likelihood estimate.
- Personnel-screening material raises privacy, equality, labour and due-process
  concerns that are not resolved by the document. Wiki retains only lawful,
  proportionate, role-based governance objectives and does not reproduce Table
  6.1 screening attributes.
- The password box at printed p. 37 contains dated construction/rotation advice.
  It is excluded pending reconciliation with current cybersecurity standards.
- Detailed high-consequence criteria, misuse-enabling examples, facility/access
  details, emergency-destruction priorities and transport procedures are not
  consolidated into public topic pages.
- DTRA financially supported the project; WHO states the content need not reflect
  US Government policy. WHO reports no disclosed interests it judged relevant
  (printed pp. x–xi; PDF pp. 12–13).
