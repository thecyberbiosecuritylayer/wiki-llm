## Edition, publication, effect and current status

The artifact remains titled *Canadian Biosafety Standard, Third Edition,
2022*. It was published in November 2022, came into effect on 1 April 2023 and
superseded the 2015 Second Edition. PHAC and CFIA made minor updates in December
2024 to align terminology and clarify biosecurity concepts; no granular public
change log was located, so this page does not infer which individual sentences
changed.

Addendum continue to apply pending alignment with the HPTA as amended on
26 March 2026. The Addendum is a distinct instrument, effective 5 January 2026
for applicable Risk Group 4/Containment Level 4 licences and permits; it is not

## Standard, law and guidance hierarchy

The preface and Figure 1 put HPTA/HPTR and HAA/HAR enabling legislation above
instruments supporting legislation, biosafety requirements, policy documents
and risk-communication/technical documents. CBS sits in the `Biosafety
Requirements` tier. Guidance and technical documents sit below it and summarize
recommendations/scientific information. The hierarchy therefore distinguishes
a national standard from statutes/regulations and from nonbinding guidance.

## Scope, adoption mechanism and exceptions

CBS sets minimum physical-containment, operational-practice and performance/
verification-testing requirements for facilities handling or storing regulated
RG2–RG4 human or terrestrial-animal pathogens or toxins. Applicable HPTA
licence and HAR permit holders must comply through licence/permit conditions.
The scope also differentiates aquatic-animal/plant-pest standards and describes
exclusions or licence exemptions for natural-environment primary specimens,
detection-only activity that does not increase quantity/concentration, specified
diagnostic work and bounded veterinary practice.

An exclusion or exemption is not a statement that no biosafety obligation or
other law applies. CBS may remain a best-practice reference, and facilities
still within HPTA but exempt from licensing retain the stated reasonable-
precautions obligation.

## Regulators, duty holders and institutional actors

PHAC is the national biosafety/biosecurity authority for human pathogens and
toxins and shares differentiated animal-pathogen responsibilities with CFIA.
Licence and permit holders remain responsible for understanding applicable law
and conditions. Within facilities, senior management, supervisors, the BSO and
personnel participate in the programme. Requirement 4.1.2 assigns the BSO
regulator communication, compliance monitoring, training records, inspections/
audits, written escalation of unresolved non-compliance and incident support.

## Institutional programme and assurance design

Section 4.1 requires a documented, implemented, maintained and evaluated
biosafety programme, risk assessments, manual, plans and procedures. Requirement
4.1.8 requires a biosecurity plan covering dual-use risks, physical/personnel
security, material accountability, inventory control, incident/emergency
response, information management and training/awareness. Its explanatory text
mentions exercise/evaluation and senior-management approval. Chapter 5 separately
sets performance and verification testing requirements.

## Evidence and scope limits

CBS covers research, teaching, public-health, diagnostic, production and animal
facility contexts within its regulated scope, but it does not use `biobank`,
`biorepository` or `repository` as a distinct governance class. A facility or
collection may fall within CBS only through actual regulated-material/activity
facts. The artifact is normative design, not adoption census or outcome study.
