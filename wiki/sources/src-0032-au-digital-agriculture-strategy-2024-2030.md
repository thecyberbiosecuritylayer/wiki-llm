## Publication and current implementation boundary

The PDF cover says October 2023 and the strategy period is 2024–2030. The AU
landing page dates its publication record 2024-02-12 and remained available on
2026-07-12. The FAO Regional Office for Africa says FAO and AUC co-hosted a
2025-06-18 implementation-support webinar with more than 200 government,
regional, private-sector, technical and research participants. It describes
the strategy as guidance for AU Member States and Regional Economic
Communities (RECs), says digital-agriculture progress across the continent is
uneven and lists support, alignment, investment and partnership needs. It is
not a measurement of DAS implementation and does not say that every Member
State adopted or implemented the strategy.

## Scope, assets and stakeholders

The strategy covers crops, livestock, fisheries and agricultural value chains,
with land, soil, water, weather/climate, plant/crop health, biodiversity and
natural resources as relevant environmental states. It names production and
food outputs; inputs such as water, fertilizer and pesticides; farm equipment,
sensors and communication infrastructure; farmer and animal identifiers;
registries/profiles; agronomic, production, weather, pest/disease, traceability,
certification, payment, insurance and model data; and national data pools and
knowledge platforms.

Its stakeholder pyramid and ecosystem map include smallholder farmers,
livestock keepers, fisherfolk, cooperatives, extension/advisory services,
agrodealers and off-takers, transport/storage, processors, marketers and
consumers, the private sector, civil society, researchers, multilateral
partners, AUC/AUDA-NEPAD, RECs and Member-State ministries. Veterinary access
appears in an illustrative advisory example, but veterinarians and veterinary/
plant laboratories are not developed as complete managed classes. Seeds,
genomes, biological samples and named pathogens are also not complete asset
classes in this source.

## Agricultural and data lifecycles

The source describes or diagrams production/monitoring, advisory and training,
input delivery, farm and breeding-cycle planning, aggregation/collection,
quality control, transformation/processing, storage and cooling, transport,
marketing, market access, finance/insurance, traceability/certification and
farm-to-fork product tracking. Crop, livestock and environmental monitoring
include pest/disease early warning, animal identification and disease zones,
crop-health observation and irrigation/fertilization decisions.

It also describes a data lifecycle: survey, sensor or human entry; digitization;
validation and metadata/standards; storage, archival and updating; sharing,
interoperability and aggregation; analysis; decision/advisory or machine
feedback. It does not supply a complete diagnostic-laboratory, treatment,
regulated animal-movement, recall, disposition or end-of-life workflow.

## System classes, dependencies and boundaries

The strategy's functional stack spans accessibility, electricity, devices,
mobile/fibre/satellite and local networks, connected farm sensing/equipment/
automation, explicit IoT and SCADA examples, drones and remote sensing,
IDs/RFID/QR/GPS/GIS, databases and central servers, cloud/PaaS, native/web/
mobile apps, SMS/USSD/IVR, analytics/DSS, AI/ML and traceability, registry,
advisory, market, insurance and early-warning platforms. One explicit
functional chain is `sensor → transmission/network → database/server/cloud →
analytics → person or machine feedback`.

Candidate boundaries include intermittent/offline operation↔periodic sync,
farm/edge↔network/cloud, data provider/owner↔platform/user, national↔regional/
continental platforms, agriculture ministry↔ICT/infrastructure authority and
producer↔private/value-chain actors. These are conceptual dependencies and
responsibility layers, not validated trust zones or a site topology.

## Functional cross-domain transfer

The source directly describes environmental, plant, soil, water, weather and
animal observations entering digital systems through satellite, drone, ground
sensor or human input. Transmission and analytics can produce forecasts,
pest/disease detection, alerts, advisories or instructions to a farmer or
machine. Intended digital outputs can in turn influence irrigation,
fertilization, pesticide use, equipment operation, certification, market
access, subsidy/voucher, credit and insurance decisions.

This is strong functional evidence for biological/environmental/sensor input→
digital decision and digital decision→physical/economic action. It is not an
observed cyberattack, malicious mechanism or proof that an untrusted digital
state caused biological harm.

## Risk, hazard and control evidence

The strategy discusses digital exclusion, access/connectivity, affordability,
data ownership/privacy, data quality, missing metadata, outdated/erroneous data
and resulting poor decisions; over-irrigation and environmental/resource risk;
climate, weather, pest and disease disruption; and supply/value-chain
constraints. It calls for validation, metadata, interoperability, privacy and
cybersecurity standards, traceability/certification, pest/disease early warning,
data platforms, planned monitoring and evaluation, coordination and resilience.

These are design risks and proposed safeguards. No adversary model, concrete
cyber vulnerability, exploit, primary cyber incident, deployed response/
recovery procedure, exercise, control test or independent effectiveness result
is reported.

## Governance, implementation plan and internal conflicts

The document assigns continental actions to AU/AUC/AUDA-NEPAD, regional
actions to RECs and national actions to Member States, with private-sector,
civil-society, research, extension and producer roles. Its seven strategic
objectives A–G cover infrastructure, national adoption, enabling environment,
human capital, knowledge/networking, reach/use and financing. Activities
include national strategies, data platforms, traceability, privacy/
cybersecurity standards, treaty/framework integration, cross-ministry task
forces, climate and pest/disease platforms, targets, dates and estimated
resources.

The plan is prospective. Its theory of change is assumption-based and its time
plan has preparation, rollout and consolidation horizons. The text contains
material internal defects:

- the document-family page says 146 pages at printed p. 2 (physical p. 8),
  although the file has 145; Table of Figures has `Error! Bookmark not
  defined.` for Figure 20 at roman p. iv (physical p. 4); the Executive Summary
  wrongly calls Chapter 6 the implementation plan at printed p. 5 (physical
  p. 11), although Chapter 5 is implementation and Chapter 6 conclusion;
- `Error! Reference source not found` remains at printed p. 27 (physical p.
  33), and invalid `Chapter 0` appears at printed p. 37 (physical p. 43);
- seven objectives A–G are introduced at printed p. 29 (physical p. 35), but
  the conclusion calls them six pillars at printed p. 40 (physical p. 46);
- printed p. 6 (physical p. 12) says about USD 165 million on the continental
  level plus national/private resources; printed p. 38 (physical p. 44) says
  USD 11.8 million continental, USD 1.8 million per REC and up to USD 2.4
  million per country, totaling up to USD 142 million; printed p. 40 (physical
  p. 46) instead says USD 10.7 million, USD 1.2 million per REC and up to USD
  2.65 million per country, totaling USD 165 million. No canonical budget or
  appropriation is asserted;
- use-case examples are admitted to be a random selection at printed p. 81
  (physical p. 87); national-strategy cards include draft, expired-period and
  non-DAS instruments at printed pp. 73–75 (physical pp. 79–81); cluster
  tables contain malformed/duplicated country entries at printed pp. 86, 89,
  91 and 93–94 (physical pp. 92, 95, 97 and 99–100).

The 42-activity A–G matrix has at least seven blank KPI cells, two literal
`X number` placeholders, output rather than outcome measures, unexplained
country/budget bases and large unestimated scopes. It supplies no baseline,
measurement method, data owner, cadence, audit/assurance, corrective-action
rule, RACI or independent evaluation. C/R/N labels action level, not an
accountable-owner or enforcement design.

Neither the current AU page nor the dated FAO companion reports completion of
the represented 2025
milestones for solar-irrigation and insurance studies, cyber/privacy minimum
standards, a free-data guidebook, an e-advisory study or a climate-data
platform. Absence in those records is not proof that no external work exists;
it prevents this transaction from claiming milestone attainment.

Figures 16–24 use 2000–2021 inputs and provide no update schedule or consistent
uncertainty/no-data method. Figure 21 has a mismatched energy-report footnote,
Figure 23 points to FAOSTAT rather than FINDEX, and Figure 24's percentage-of-
GNI caption conflicts with a US-dollar legend. These maps support historical
prioritization context only, not current 2026 readiness, adoption or KPI
attainment.

## Independence, integration and safety boundary

The AU/AUC/PRIDA regional strategy is a distinct African strategy lineage and
candidate independent evidence relative to the U.S.-centered Drape workshop
(`SRC-0010`), the U.S. National One Health Framework (`SRC-0020`) and the
global Quadripartite national guide (`SRC-0021`). Independence is claim-
specific: FAO/ITU and other organizations participated in or are cited by the
strategy, and Figure 2, readiness inputs, technology/use-case landscape and
methods draw on third-party work. The PDF, AU landing page and co-hosted FAO/
AUC companion remain one coordinated AU transaction rather than three
lineages. Cited studies, commercial examples and country cards are not imported
as evidence.

The document never uses One Health as its organizing framework. It does not
complete veterinary/plant laboratory, treatment/movement, incident,
vulnerability, response/recovery or measured-effectiveness evidence. Only a
later synthesis may test the claim-specific union of distinct sources against frozen AGE
criteria; this source transaction changes no rubric cell.
